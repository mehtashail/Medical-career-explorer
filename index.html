Your mission is to explore—not decide

At age 12, you do not need to choose a specialty or build a “perfect pre-med résumé.” Your job is to learn what different health professionals do, strengthen your school skills, help people, and notice which types of work make you curious.

Week 1

Inside the human body

3–4 hours
Mini lesson: Human Body Theater: Body systems

Human Body Theater: Body systems

Read the body-systems pages in Human Body Theater. An organ system is a group of organs that work together. The respiratory system brings oxygen into the lungs. The circulatory system carries oxygen and nutrients through the body. The digestive system breaks food into nutrients, and muscles use oxygen and nutrients for movement.
What do you remember?
1. What is an organ system?
2. Which system brings oxygen into the lungs?
3. What does the circulatory system carry?
Mini lesson: Heart and blood-pressure videos

Heart and blood-pressure videos

Use the assigned heart and blood-pressure videos. The heart is a muscular pump. Arteries usually carry blood away from the heart, and veins usually return blood to it. Blood pressure is the force of blood pushing against blood-vessel walls.
What do you remember?
1. What is the heart's main job?
2. Which vessels usually carry blood away from the heart?
3. What is blood pressure?
Mini lesson: Human Body Theater: Circulation

Human Body Theater: Circulation

Read the heart and circulation section in Human Body Theater. The heart's chambers and valves help blood move in the correct direction. Arteries usually carry blood away from the heart. Veins usually return it. Capillaries allow oxygen, nutrients, and wastes to move between blood and tissues.
What do you remember?
1. What helps blood move in the correct direction?
2. Which vessels usually return blood to the heart?
3. What happens in capillaries?
Mini lesson: Questions that grow from reading

Questions that grow from reading

After reading a body-system chapter, write questions about ideas the text introduced but did not fully explain. A strong question is specific, clear, and connected to the reading. For example: “How does the body know when to make breathing faster?”
What do you remember?
1. Where should an 'I wonder' question come from?
2. What makes a question strong?
3. Which question fits a heart-and-lungs reading?
Week 2

Meet the healthcare team

3–5 hours
Mini lesson: NIH Health Quest careers

NIH Health Quest careers

NIH Health Quest introduces middle-school students to health-science careers through career videos, role-model interviews, and a story-based mission where students help solve a scientific problem or answer an important research question.

Complete these exact steps:
  1. Choose one biomedical research career. Record what disease, cell, gene, molecule, or body process the person studies.
  2. Choose one behavioral research career. Record what behavior, decision, emotion, habit, or community factor the person studies.
  3. Choose one clinical research career. Record how the person studies health or treatments involving patients or patient information.
  4. For all three careers, record: main job, workplace, education, tools or evidence used, and one surprising fact.
  5. Imagine a Health Quest mission about a public-health outbreak. Assign each of the three professionals one job on the team.
  6. Choose the career that interests you most and explain which part of the NIH video or interview influenced your choice.
The goal is to connect each career to the kind of health-science problem it helps investigate—not just to list job facts.
What do you remember?
1. What three research areas are featured in the NIH Health Quest career resources?
2. Which career is most likely to study cells, genes, molecules, or body processes?
3. Which researcher might study how habits, emotions, or community conditions affect health?
4. How does the Health Quest game connect careers to science?
Mini lesson: Career interview preparation

Career interview preparation

Before interviewing a healthcare professional, prepare questions about a normal day, training, favorite responsibilities, challenges, and advice for students. Listen carefully and write notes afterward. A parent or guardian should help arrange the conversation.
What do you remember?
1. What should you do before the interview?
2. Which is a useful question?
3. Who should help arrange it?
Mini lesson: Career scorecard

Career scorecard

A career scorecard compares careers using the same categories and scoring scale. Categories can include people contact, teamwork, science, creativity, schedule, and training length. It helps you notice patterns in your interests, but it does not choose a career for you.
What do you remember?
1. Why use the same categories?
2. Which could be a category?
3. What does the scorecard do?
Mini lesson: The whole healthcare team

The whole healthcare team

Hospitals include many professionals besides physicians: nurses, pharmacists, respiratory therapists, physical therapists, laboratory scientists, imaging technologists, social workers, dietitians, and others. Some work directly with patients; others provide tests, technology, or support.
What do you remember?
1. Who contributes to hospital care?
2. Who may help with breathing?
3. Do all roles work directly with patients?
Week 3

Think like a researcher

4–5 hours
Mini lesson: How researchers form questions

How researchers form questions

Researchers begin by reading what is already known. Then they identify something uncertain. A good research question is clear, focused, and answerable with evidence. It should not assume the answer. Research involving people requires special rules, supervision, and ethical review.
What do you remember?
1. What should researchers do first?
2. What makes a good question?
3. What does research with people require?
Mini lesson: Parts of a research study

Parts of a research study

A study begins with a question. A hypothesis is a reasoned prediction. A variable is something that changes or is measured. Results report what the data showed. A conclusion explains what the results may mean. Limitations describe factors that may have affected the study.
What do you remember?
1. What is a hypothesis?
2. What do results report?
3. What are limitations?
Mini lesson: Why research rules matter

Why research rules matter

Research rules protect people and make findings trustworthy. Safety reduces risk. Fairness reduces bias. Privacy protects personal information. Honest recording means keeping accurate results, including unexpected ones.
What do you remember?
1. What does privacy protect?
2. What should happen to unexpected results?
3. How does fairness help?
Mini lesson: Tables and graphs

Tables and graphs

Tables organize exact values in rows and columns. Graphs make patterns easier to see. Every graph should have a clear title, labeled axes, and units when measurements are used. Bar graphs compare categories. Line graphs often show change over time.
What do you remember?
1. What do tables organize?
2. What should every graph include?
3. Which graph often shows change over time?

Mini lesson: Service that supports health

Service that supports health

Students can support community health by helping at a food pantry, assembling hygiene kits, tutoring, or making health-literacy materials from reliable sources. Work should be supervised by a responsible adult or organization. Students should not diagnose people or tell them to change medicine.
What do you remember?
1. Which is appropriate?
2. What should health materials use?
3. What should students avoid?
Mini lesson: CPR and first aid

CPR and first aid

CPR and first aid should be learned from qualified instructors. A certified class teaches correct technique, safety, and when to call emergency services. Reading helps you understand the purpose, but rescue techniques require proper instruction and supervision.
What do you remember?
1. Who should teach CPR?
2. What does a certified class teach?
3. Is reading alone enough to practice rescue techniques?
Mini lesson: Museum or virtual exhibit

Museum or virtual exhibit

Before visiting a science museum or virtual exhibit, prepare two questions. During the visit, record three facts and one surprising exhibit. Afterward, explain how the experience connected to something you learned in the book.
What do you remember?
1. What should you prepare first?
2. What should you record?
3. What should you do afterward?
Mini lesson: Five-minute grand rounds

Five-minute grand rounds

Your presentation should explain what you explored, one important lesson, three careers that interested you, what surprised you, and what you want to learn next. A clear presentation has an opening, organized main points, and a conclusion.
What do you remember?
1. What should the presentation include?
2. How many careers should you discuss?
3. What makes the presentation clear?

Safe, fun projects to try

These projects are designed for exploration, not diagnosis. A parent or guardian should supervise activities, visits and professional interviews.

❤️

Pulse & recovery investigation

Question: How long does it take your pulse to return toward its resting rate after gentle movement?

  1. Sit quietly for five minutes.
  2. Count your pulse for 30 seconds and multiply by two.
  3. Walk briskly or do another comfortable activity for two minutes.
  4. Measure immediately, then once per minute for five minutes.
  5. Graph the results and describe the pattern.

Safety: Stop if you feel pain, dizziness, breathing trouble or discomfort. Do not compare people or make health conclusions.

🧠

Reaction-time lab

Use the ruler-drop method to test your own reaction time across several trials. Calculate an average and investigate whether practice changes the result.

Only collect data from another person with parent/guardian and participant permission. Avoid collecting names or private health information.

🦾

Design a helpful medical tool

Choose a problem such as remembering medicine, opening a container with limited hand movement or communicating pain. Sketch three solutions, build a cardboard prototype and ask someone to test its usability.

🧼

Handwashing demonstration

Use washable paint, lotion with glitter or another safe material to represent contamination. Compare which hand areas are often missed before and after careful washing.

Do not culture bacteria or use bodily fluids. Microbiology projects require proper supervision and science-fair approval.

🎤

Career interview

Interview a physician, nurse, therapist, pharmacist, researcher, EMT, biomedical engineer or public-health professional.

Eight questions to ask
  1. What does a normal day look like?
  2. What do you enjoy most?
  3. What is hardest?
  4. How much time do you spend with people?
  5. Which school subjects matter most?
  6. What education and training did you complete?
  7. What surprised you about the career?
  8. What should a middle-school student try?
📣

Health communication challenge

Choose one topic from NIH or MedlinePlus and create a one-page explanation for another seventh grader. Use plain language, define unfamiliar words and cite every source.

Your page should educate, not give personal medical advice.

Important safety and ethics rules

  • Never diagnose yourself, a friend or a family member.
  • Do not change medicines, diets or exercise plans because of a project.
  • Do not use blood, saliva, unknown chemicals, cultures or dangerous equipment.
  • Ask a teacher before beginning any project involving people, microorganisms, medicines or health records.
  • Use qualified instruction for CPR, first aid and clinical skills.
  • Protect privacy: do not record names, diagnoses or personal medical details.

Long-term roadmap: middle school to medicine

This is a direction—not a checklist. Choose appropriate challenge, protect your sleep and health, and build depth in activities you genuinely care about.

Grades 7–8: Explore broadly

  • Strengthen math, science, reading, writing and speaking.
  • Complete one real investigation or engineering project.
  • Try HOSA, science fair, Science Olympiad, robotics, coding or debate if available.
  • Serve consistently in your community.
  • Interview several health professionals.
  • Keep a career-exploration journal.

Grades 9–10: Build foundations

  • Take the school’s appropriate biology, chemistry, math and writing sequence.
  • Join activities you can continue for several years.
  • Develop responsibility before chasing leadership titles.
  • At age 14+, investigate hospital and community volunteering.
  • Continue one non-medical passion such as athletics, music or art.

Grades 11–12: Deepen your work

  • Choose rigorous courses that fit your preparation and workload.
  • Seek deeper research, service, clinical exposure or health-technology work.
  • Lead through measurable contribution, not just a title.
  • Consider age-eligible programs such as Tufts Mini Med School or MGH junior volunteering.
  • Research colleges for advising, affordability, academic fit, research and service opportunities.

College and “pre-med”

Pre-med is usually not a major. Medical schools do not require or prefer one particular major. You can study biology, engineering, public health, English, economics or another subject while completing each medical school’s prerequisite courses.

During college, students commonly develop:

  • Strong performance in required science and other courses
  • Clinical exposure and understanding of patient care
  • Long-term community service
  • Research or another form of intellectual inquiry
  • Communication, teamwork, reliability and ethical judgment
  • Relationships with professors, mentors and pre-health advisers

Medical schools evaluate more than grades. The AAMC’s competency model includes professional qualities, thinking and reasoning abilities, and science skills.

Empathy Reliability Communication Critical thinking Scientific inquiry Teamwork

AAMC: choosing a major AAMC competencies

What makes a strong future college applicant? Appropriate academic challenge, sustained curiosity, meaningful service, depth in a few activities, excellent communication and evidence that you improve the communities you join. There is no single required “pre-med” activity.
